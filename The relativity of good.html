The relativity of good

Published on January 2, 2026

What exactly is evil? I often ask myself this question because I don’t understand why anyone would intentionally hurt another person. Why must we have laws? Why can’t we simply live together in peace and harmony? Why do we need barriers, punishments, rules, and restrictions? Why do some people not understand that killing, torturing, suppressing, censoring, or forcing someone to stay with them against their will is morally unacceptable? The list of harmful actions is long, but the contradiction remains — humans seem capable of both great kindness and inexplicable cruelty.

At the same time, I believe that goodness is relative. What seems right or good to one person can be interpreted as wrong by another. Moral judgments are shaped by culture, upbringing, experience, and personal psychology. There is no single universal measure of what is “good”, because individual values and social norms differ across time, space, and communities.

To understand harmful behavior, psychology provides several frameworks. One area of research examines the personality trait known as everyday sadism — a tendency to derive pleasure from witnessing or inflicting pain. Studies show that individuals high in these traits not only lack empathy but also willingly expend effort to make others suffer, and feel less distress when they believe their actions cause real harm.

Some researchers connect sadistic pleasure to reduced emotional sensitivity. In psychological studies, people with sadistic tendencies report stronger gratification when their victims appear to suffer, and this can reinforce aggressive behavior. Conversely, emerging findings suggest the pleasure may be short-lived and even replaced by negative emotion afterwards, similar to how addictive cycles work — where an initial thrill gives way to emotional crash.

Other research explores psychopathy — a condition marked by impaired empathy, shallow emotions, and a diminished capacity for guilt or remorse. Psychopaths may not only disregard others’ suffering, but some studies indicate they can feel excitement or power from harmful acts due to abnormal neural responses to fear and distress. This doesn’t mean all people who commit violence are psychopaths, but it reveals how neurological and emotional differences can influence moral perception and behavior.

There are also broader psychological theories explaining aggression that don’t assume inherent “evil”, but connect harmful acts to frustration or cognitive factors. For example, the frustration–aggression hypothesis suggests that aggression arises when individuals are blocked from achieving goals or feel intense frustration, and this emotional tension may be displaced onto others.

From a philosophical standpoint, moral relativism argues that good and evil are not absolute categories, but depend on cultural norms and individual perspectives. This means what one society considers moral, another might judge differently, and even within a single society individuals can disagree on fundamental principles. Yet relativism doesn’t justify harmful behavior — it merely highlights that moral values are shaped by context.

So why do we have laws, punishments, and barriers? Psychologically and socially, humans are capable of empathy and cooperation, but we are also capable of bias, fear, and competition. Without laws and structured systems, there is no collective agreement on boundaries that protect rights and prevent harm. Laws exist not because humans are inherently bad, but because individual actions can sometimes conflict with collective well-being, and society needs shared rules to maintain stability.

Ultimately, my conclusion is this: the nature of good and evil is complex and influenced by psychology, social norms, and personal experience. While many people strive for kindness, others may be shaped by factors that distort their moral compass — whether neurological, environmental, emotional, or cultural. But striving for empathy, understanding, and compassion — even in the face of disagreement — is one of the clearest expressions of what people throughout history have called “good”.
